UT Austin ¬∑ AI & ML Program ¬∑ Decision Tree Analysis
The Business Problem

AllLife Bank wanted to convert its liability (deposit) customers into personal loan customers. With a historically low conversion rate of ~9%, the marketing team needed a smarter way to identify who to target ‚Äî rather than blasting the entire customer base with campaigns that mostly missed.

My Approach
  • Performed thorough EDA to understand customer demographics, financial behavior, and existing product usage patterns
  • Analyzed class imbalance in the target variable and assessed its implications on model performance metrics
  • Built and compared Decision Tree classifiers using both pre-pruning (hyperparameter tuning via GridSearchCV) and post-pruning (cost-complexity pruning) strategies
  • Evaluated models using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC ‚Äî with business context guiding metric prioritization (recall matters more when false negatives are costly)
  • Identified the most predictive features driving loan conversion: income, credit card spending, education level, and CD account ownership
Key Findings
Top Predictor

Income and CC average spend were the strongest signals ‚Äî high earners with active spending were disproportionately likely to convert.

Model Performance

Post-pruned tree achieved strong recall on the minority (loan = yes) class while maintaining generalizability on unseen data.

Class Imbalance

With only ~9% positive cases, standard accuracy was misleading ‚Äî the project emphasized precision-recall tradeoffs for the business context.

Business Impact

Model recommendations could allow the bank to focus campaign spend on the ~20% of customers responsible for the majority of conversions.

üéØ

Business Recommendation

Target customers with income above $100K, higher CC spending, and existing CD accounts first. A tiered outreach strategy using model scores could improve campaign ROI by reducing wasted spend on low-probability customers.

The Business Problem

FoodHub, a food delivery aggregator in New York, was looking to understand demand patterns across its restaurant network. The goal: use order-level data to uncover which restaurants, cuisines, and timeframes drive the most volume ‚Äî and identify friction points in the customer experience (particularly delivery time and ratings).

My Approach
  • Framed the analysis around 6 stakeholder-defined questions before touching the data ‚Äî ensuring findings would directly answer business needs
  • Cleaned and explored an order-level dataset covering cuisine type, order value, delivery time, day of week, and customer ratings
  • Used univariate and multivariate analysis to identify demand concentration, delivery time distributions, and rating correlations
  • Visualized patterns across weekday vs. weekend, cuisine categories, and price tiers to surface actionable segmentation insights
  • Translated statistical findings into plain-language business recommendations without losing analytical rigor
Key Findings
Demand Concentration

A small subset of restaurants generated a disproportionate share of orders ‚Äî classic long-tail distribution with 20% of restaurants driving ~60% of volume.

Weekend vs. Weekday

Weekend demand skewed toward American and Italian cuisine; weekday patterns favored faster, lighter options ‚Äî suggesting different promotional strategies per segment.

Delivery Time & Ratings

Orders exceeding 60 minutes showed a measurable dip in ratings ‚Äî a quantifiable threshold for service level targets and restaurant accountability metrics.

Order Value Patterns

Higher-value orders were more likely on weekends and from specific cuisine types ‚Äî pointing to upsell and partnership prioritization opportunities.
